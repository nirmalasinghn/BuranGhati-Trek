The Call

We don't just call them the Himalayas… we call them the Mighty Himalayas for a reason. They don't need to shout — they call you. Quietly. Deeply. And when your heart is in the right place, with the purest of intentions, things just… come together. That's how it happened for me. No plans, no overthinking. Just a pull I couldn't ignore.

My journey began long before I stepped into the mountains. It started with 5 km every day — a test of discipline, not just fitness. But nothing truly prepares you for what's to come.

When I finally reached Himachal, even the bumpy roads, the bone-chilling breeze, and the river flowing beside me felt like a blessing.

The trek began amidst golden wheat fields, with a rucksack alongside 23 strangers. Strangers who, over time, became my tribe.
Janglik valley at dusk — the moment the mountains first started speaking to me
01
Janglik to Dayara Meadows
Drive from Rohru + First Trek  ·  9,415 ft → 11,115 ft  ·  7.5 km
A postcard from the forest — words that find you at the right altitude

We walked through wild paths, across crazy ascents, tiny flowers blooming at our feet, and grasslands scattered with boulders. Streams gushed by our side. And somehow, through the hardship, laughter, and silence… we became a family.

Every campsite felt earned. When we reached our first campsite, Dayara — meadows filled with flowers — every trail looked like it was pulled straight out of a Windows wallpaper. Surreal. There were cows, goats, horses, and a dog that followed us everywhere.

I felt like the main character in a story — no network, no distractions. Just me, nature, and the peace of true solitude.

Day 2 · Litham

The Sacred River.
The Shift I Didn't Expect.

Then came DIY day. A day where we did everything — cooked, cleaned, navigated using maps, read contour lines, and answered questions like: "Still how far?", "When's the next break?", "Can I rest now?" It was real, raw, and deeply humbling.

We pushed through rocks and heat and finally reached Litham — our second campsite. There, I sat beside the sacred Chandranahan river and meditated. When I opened my eyes to see the snow-capped peaks, I felt something shift. A kind of happiness that can't be explained. Only felt.

We lived in the same clothes for days, shared food, played crazy games, survived on the bare minimum. And in that simplicity, I learned something profound — you don't need much to be content.

No complaints. No demands. Just a peaceful, symbiotic rhythm with nature.

A shepherd's stone hut at Litham — prayer flags dancing, a horse grazing at golden hour. This is someone's whole life.
03
Chandranahan Lake — The Glacial Dream
Acclimatisation hike  ·  11,485 ft → 13,020 ft  ·  6.9 km round trip

Then came the Chandranahan Lake — a one-day, 2,000 ft ascent and descent. Tough, yes. But the beauty? Unmatched. The lake, glowing in the sun, fed by glacial melt… and a pawed companion walking loyally beside me. It was cinematic.

Nights were magical. We'd lie under the stars, learning about constellations, silent… until that moment — my first shooting star. I'll never forget that. 💫

Snowfield smiles near Chandranahan — 23 strangers, one family. These faces became home at 12,000 ft.
Nataraja on snow — even the mountains need yoga energy ✨
The sky above Litham — where I saw my first shooting star.
04
Dhunda — Where the Fog Swallows Everything
Litham → Dhunda  ·  11,485 ft → 13,425 ft  ·  4.6 km

Acclimatization at 13,000 ft followed, at a place called Dhunda — which literally means "foggy." And believe me, you couldn't see a soul standing in front of you. That fog swallowed everything. Every tent, every voice, every footstep — absorbed into silence.

It should have been unsettling. Instead, it was the most at peace I had ever felt. Sitting in that cloud, knowing tomorrow was the big one.

Summit Day — Day 5

3 AM. Headlamps on.
Into the Unknown.

We woke up at 1:30 AM. Outside, it was pitch dark. 0 degrees. At 3 AM, our headlamps flickered on, and we began our silent ascent into the unknown.

The trail was brutal. Rocks, boulders, black ice. One wrong step and you'd fall 1,000 feet straight back to camp. But then, there were butterflies — dancing around us like the universe was cheering us on.

Just when my body said, "Enough," I looked up… and saw the summit. 15,000 feet. I made it. I felt proud. Really proud.

I patted myself on the back, picked up a rock as a memory, and began the descent — 8 km of pure test. Steep slopes, a narrow 2-foot-wide rockfall section, and icy slides — some intentional, some not. We laughed, we screamed, we slipped, and we held on.

At one point, we were so exhausted, we just dropped. Right there on the grass and rocks. In the rain. Sleeping like babies.

15,073 ft. The Garmin doesn't lie. This number changed something in me forever.
At the top of Buran Ghati Pass — windswept, breathless, magnificently alive. My first 15,000 ft summit.
06
River Campsite — The Last Night
After the pass  ·  10,745 ft  ·  The final camp before Barua

And finally, we reached River Campsite. The last one. That night, we celebrated the toughest day of the trek. The sound of the river, the mountain air, and the people around me… we weren't strangers anymore. We were something more.

That journey stripped us of ego. It made space for self-reflection, gratitude, and growth.

Mountains make people realize how small our problems and fears really are — and there is so much left to explore in this world.

Because it wasn't just a trek. It's the way of life for me now.

A Night to Remember

My First
Shooting Star

We lay under the Himalayan sky, learning constellations, speaking softly, then not at all. And then — a streak of light across the darkness. In that one moment, everything I had ever worried about dissolved completely into stardust.

Some experiences can't be photographed. Only lived.
